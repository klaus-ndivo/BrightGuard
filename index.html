How This Project Aligns with the Assessment Brief

With reference to scholarly material, this site has been designed to address adversity related to harmful sexual behaviour among young people in Australia, and to show clearly how an educator might understand and respond to this adversity.

  1. 1. Prevalence in Australia. Across the topic pages we highlight how online harassment, pornography exposure, coercive sexting and unsafe consent practices are increasingly reported among Australian children and adolescents, drawing on developmental and educational research (Duchesne, McMaugh & Mackenzie, 2022; McDevitt & Ormrod, 2010).
  2. 2. Characteristics and signals. Each topic page identifies observable signals that a student may be living with this adversity—for example secrecy online, changes in mood, boundary-testing with peers, pressure to share images, or withdrawal from learning and friendships (Green & Piel, 2010).
  3. 3. Implications for development and learning. The project explains how harmful sexual behaviours can affect healthy social–emotional development, identity, and classroom engagement, and how shame, anxiety and trauma can interfere with concentration, behaviour and achievement (Margetts & Woolfolk, 2019; McInerney, 2014).
  4. 4. Students at higher risk. Content on guidance, support and safety pages notes that some students are at increased risk or vulnerability—such as those with prior trauma, disability or special learning needs, and Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander students who may experience ongoing discrimination and intergenerational disadvantage.
  5. 5. Range of impacts for Aboriginal and special needs students. We emphasise that adversity can compound existing barriers to participation, sense of belonging and access to support, and that responses must be culturally safe, inclusive, and tailored to individual strengths and needs, in partnership with families and community services.
  6. 6. Educator actions and critical reflection. Throughout the guidance, consent, harassment and safety pages, we model what educators can do at a whole‑school level, in classroom practice, and through family–school partnerships to prevent harm, notice early warning signs, respond restoratively, and link students and families to community support (Duchesne, McMaugh & Mackenzie, 2022; McDevitt & Ormrod, 2010).
