Definition

How Harmful Sexual Behaviour Develops

Children can develop harmful sexual behaviour when unsafe sexual actions are rewarded (with attention, social status, or peers‚Äô submission) or when they remain unchallenged. Signals That a Student May Be Experiencing or Displaying HSB

Students living with this adversity may exhibit a wide range of signals, depending on whether they are experiencing harm, displaying HSB, or both.

Behavioural

  • Sexualised behaviours inconsistent with developmental stage.
  • Increased aggression, defiance, or difficulty regulating impulses (Duchesne, McMaugh & Mackenzie 2022).
  • Compulsive device use and secrecy online.

Emotional

  • Heightened anxiety, shame, withdrawal, or low mood.
  • Sudden changes in self‚Äëesteem after online incidents.
  • Discomfort in peer interactions.

Social

  • Friendships breaking down due to sexual comments.
  • Involvement in risky peer groups.
  • Persistent boundary‚Äëtesting or ignoring consent rules.

Cognitive & Learning

  • Sudden decline in academic performance or focus.
  • Fixation on sexual themes in class tasks.
  • Preoccupation with online content during lessons.

Implications for Healthy Development and Learning

HSB has profound implications across emotional, social, and cognitive development.

Emotional development

  • Chronic shame, guilt, or confusion about sexuality.
  • Emotional dysregulation that disrupts relationships and school engagement.
  • Increased risk of anxiety, depression and other mental‚Äëhealth difficulties.

Cognitive development

Piaget‚Äôs Cognitive Development Theory highlights that children interpret experiences through age‚Äërelated thinking patterns. Early exposure to explicit content can disrupt age‚Äëappropriate understandings of relationships, consent and power, leading to distorted beliefs.

Students at High Risk

Certain groups are more vulnerable to experiencing or displaying HSB because of structural, developmental or contextual factors:

  • Children exposed to family violence, trauma or sexual abuse.
  • Students in out‚Äëof‚Äëhome care or unstable housing.
  • Young people with disabilities, especially intellectual disability or ASD.
  • Students with insecure or disrupted attachment histories.
  • Adolescents with limited supervision or very high online autonomy.
  • Students in peer groups that normalise misogyny or sexualised bullying.

These students may struggle to build safe relationships, receive less consistent guidance, or rely heavily on peers and media for information about sex and relationships, which can increase vulnerability to both harm and harmful behaviour.

Impact on Students with Special Needs

Students with disabilities often face heightened vulnerability to HSB because they may:

  • Find it harder to understand social cues or sexual boundaries.
  • Be more exposed to online exploitation or manipulation.
  • Experience communication barriers that make disclosure difficult.
  • Be at increased risk of being both victims and perpetrators.

Impacts can include greater emotional distress, social isolation, increased behavioural challenges, and lower participation in school activities due to stigma or exclusion.

What Educators Can Do

Supporting students living with or engaging in HSB requires a holistic, child‚Äëcentred, trauma‚Äëinformed and culturally responsive approach.

  • Establish clear, developmentally appropriate expectations around respect and consent.
  • Use restorative and educative responses rather than purely punitive approaches.
  • Work closely with families, allied health professionals and safeguarding teams.
  • Ensure classrooms include diverse, positive role models and inclusive curricula.

Educators can model calm, respectful responses, notice early warning signs, and help students and families navigate support pathways before harm escalates.
